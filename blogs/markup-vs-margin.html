Markup vs Margin: Don’t Mix These Up

Most businesses lose money because they calculate profit wrong. The biggest mistake? Confusing markup with margin.

If you price products using the wrong number, you might think you’re making “30% profit”… but in reality you’re making far less.

Quick definitions (simple)

Markup = Profit ÷ Cost
Margin = Profit ÷ Selling Price

Both are useful, but they are NOT the same.

One simple example

Let’s say:

  • Cost price = ₹100
  • Selling price = ₹150
  • Profit = ₹50
Metric Formula Answer
Markup ₹50 ÷ ₹100 50%
Margin ₹50 ÷ ₹150 33.33%

Same product. Same profit. But two different percentages. That’s why mixing them up creates pricing mistakes.

The common mistake (that kills profit)

Many small business owners say: “I want 30% profit. So I’ll add 30% on cost.”

Adding 30% on cost gives you 30% MARKUP — not 30% MARGIN.

If your goal is 30% margin, your markup needs to be higher than 30%. Otherwise your net profit will be lower than expected.

How to convert markup ↔ margin

Markup → Margin

Margin % = Markup % ÷ (100 + Markup %)

Example: Markup = 50%
Margin = 50 ÷ 150 = 33.33%

Margin → Markup

Markup % = Margin % ÷ (100 − Margin %)

Example: Margin = 30%
Markup = 30 ÷ 70 = 42.86%

Which one should you use in business?

Simple rule:

  • Use Markup when you’re setting a selling price from cost.
  • Use Margin when you’re checking business profitability.

Why margin matters more for profit tracking: because your rent, salaries, marketing, and taxes come from your selling price revenue.

Real-world pricing tip

If you sell on platforms (Amazon/Flipkart/Swiggy/Zomato) or do shipping, your real cost is not just product cost.

True Cost = Product cost + packaging + delivery + platform fee + payment fee + returns risk

Always calculate markup/margin on the true cost. Otherwise you’ll feel like “sales are high” but cash is not growing.
