Markup vs Margin: Don’t Mix These Up

Most businesses lose money because they calculate profit wrong. The biggest mistake? Confusing markup with margin.

If you price products using the wrong number, you might think you’re making “30% profit”… but in reality, you’re making far less.

Quick definitions (simple)

Markup = Profit ÷ Cost
Margin = Profit ÷ Selling Price

Both are useful, but they are NOT the same.

One simple example

Let’s say:

  • Cost price = ₹100
  • Selling price = ₹150
  • Profit = ₹50
Metric Formula Answer
Markup ₹50 ÷ ₹100 50%
Margin ₹50 ÷ ₹150 33.33%

Same product. Same profit. But two different percentages. That’s why mixing them up creates massive pricing mistakes.

The common mistake (that kills profit)

Many small business owners say: “I want 30% profit. So I’ll add 30% on cost.”

⚠️ Warning: Adding 30% on cost gives you a 30% MARKUP — not a 30% MARGIN.

If your goal is a 30% margin, your markup needs to be higher than 30%. Otherwise, your net profit at the end of the month will be unexpectedly low.

How to convert markup ↔ margin

Markup → Margin

Margin % = Markup % ÷ (100 + Markup %)

Example: Markup = 50%
Margin = 50 ÷ 150 = 33.33%

Margin → Markup

Markup % = Margin % ÷ (100 − Margin %)

Example: Margin = 30%
Markup = 30 ÷ 70 = 42.86%

Which one should you use in business?

Simple rule:

  • Use Markup when you’re setting a selling price starting from your cost.
  • Use Margin when you’re checking the overall profitability of your business.

Why margin matters more for tracking: Your rent, salaries, marketing, and taxes are all paid out of your total revenue (the selling price), not your cost.

Real-world pricing tip

If you sell on platforms (Amazon/Flipkart/Swiggy/Zomato) or ship physical goods, your real cost is not just the product cost.

True Cost = Product cost + packaging + delivery + platform fee + payment fee + returns risk

Always calculate your markup and margin on the true cost. Otherwise, you’ll feel like “sales are high” but wonder why the bank account isn't growing.
